🎬 Prologue — Pick your machine

Decisions made once, before any experiment runs: where the data lives, and which door your team walks through.

🏦 Warehouse-Native vs Cloud Experimentation

Cloud: the vendor hosts a copy of your dataWarehouse-native: analysis runs INSIDE your warehouse
🧒 ELI6: Cloud = you send your groceries to the vendor's kitchen. They store a copy, cook the analysis, and plate the results. Warehouse-native = the chef comes to YOUR kitchen. The recipes (SQL queries) travel to your warehouse, the cooking happens next to your fridge, and only the finished tasting notes go back. Your groceries never leave the building — and your fridge holds ingredients the vendor's kitchen has never seen: refunds, margins, offline orders.
✓ Your cloud mental model — confirmed. Target & bucket users → send a key to the app so it knows what to render → stream events up → stats engine + duration estimators crunch → significance on the dashboard. That's cloud, and it's correct. Warehouse-native changes WHERE the crunching happens, not WHAT it computes. Bucketing and the key to the app work exactly the same (SDK — or even your own assignment system). But the metric data and the statistics move into your warehouse: the engine ships SQL in, runs the math beside your tables, and only a tiny results summary (typically <1,000 rows) comes back out.
💄 AmpliBeauty: The CFO doesn't trust "revenue" from SDK purchase events — real net revenue lives in Snowflake finance tables, after refunds, wholesale exclusions and marketplace fees. And legal won't let raw order data leave the company. Warehouse-native squares it: the free-shipping-threshold experiment still assigns variants through the SDK, but the success metric is net revenue after returns, computed inside Snowflake from the same tables the board dashboard uses. The CFO signs off because the numbers reconcile by construction. The trade: results refresh on a schedule (nightly reloads), not in real time.

Cloud experimentation

  • SDK assigns variants and streams events to the vendor's cloud, which stores its own copy of the data.
  • Stats engine runs on that copy; results are near-real-time.
  • Fast to start, no data team required — but metrics are limited to what the SDK tracked.

Warehouse-native (e.g. Statsig WHN)

  • You grant a service user access you control; the engine writes SQL pipelines into Snowflake / BigQuery / Databricks and computes there.
  • Metrics defined on your tables via a semantic layer — finance revenue, returns, margin, even offline data.
  • Only small result sets (~<1,000 rows) leave the warehouse; exposure data retained max 30 days vendor-side.
  • Trades: scheduled batch reloads instead of real-time, and warehouse compute costs are yours.

🎨 Web Experiment vs 🔧 Feature Experiment

Web: no-code visual editor, websites onlyFeature: SDK flags, client + server, the full toolkit
🧒 ELI6: Web Experiment is a sticker kit — a marketer opens a visual editor, points at the live website, swaps a headline or an image, tests a redirect. No engineer, no release, live by lunch. Feature Experiment is a workshop — engineers wire variants into the actual code behind feature flags, on any surface including the backend. Stickers change the surface; the workshop rebuilds the machine. Rule of thumb: can you describe the change by pointing at a webpage? Sticker kit. Does it need a pull request? Workshop.
💄 AmpliBeauty: Marketing A/B tests the homepage headline — "Glow up your routine" vs "Skincare that works" — in the visual editor and it's live the same morning: Web Experiment. The product team tests the new Shade Finder quiz flow in the iOS app plus the checkout API behind it: Feature Experiment, because there's no sticker for a quiz engine. Same platform, same stats — different door in.

🎨 Web Experiment

  • Visual editor + URL targeting + redirect tests; runs on websites via a browser snippet tuned for anti-flicker.
  • Built for marketers/growth — launch without a release cycle.
  • No sticky bucketing; a focused subset of the toolkit.

🔧 Feature Experiment

  • SDK/flag-based, code-level variants on web, mobile and backend — this is where the whole client/server × local/remote 2×2 lives.
  • Deployments, sticky bucketing, testers, flag dependencies, assignment/exposure charts.
  • Needs engineering, buys you unlimited scope.

🎲 Act 1 — Yi Shiean gets sorted

Follow one shopper: the instant she arrives, deterministic math decides her variant — and keeps deciding it the same way, forever.

🪣 Bucketing

Deterministic hash: bucketing key + salt → variantDefault key: Amplitude ID
🧒 ELI6: A sorting hat that never changes its mind. It reads your name, does some secret math, and puts you in a house. Same name = same house, every single time — on your phone, your laptop, or after you reinstall. There's no notebook of past decisions; the math itself IS the memory.
Hash(user + salt) → a number 0–99 → variant range
💄 AmpliBeauty: Shopper Yi Shiean opens the Shade Finder test on her phone at lunch, her laptop at night, and after reinstalling the app next week. hash(Yi Shiean's ID + this flag's salt) always lands on 37 → always treatment. Across 2M shoppers, nobody stores 2M assignments — the formula recomputes the same answer instantly, anywhere.

How it works in Amplitude

  • Bucketing key (default Amplitude ID; can be user property or group for B2B) + the flag's salt go into a hash → a bucket number → mapped to variant allocation ranges.
  • No assignment storage needed — deterministic and stateless.
  • Anonymous users bucketed by device can "variant jump" after identity merge — a classic gotcha.

Gotchas to know

  • Anonymous users bucketed by device can variant jump once they log in and identities merge.
  • B2B tip: bucket by company ID so everyone in an org sees the same variant.

🧂 Bucketing Salt

The "reshuffle" ingredient in the hashAdvanced Settings on the flag
🧒 ELI6: The sorting hat's secret ingredient. Keep the same salt and the sorting never changes. Change the salt and the entire school gets re-sorted — everyone could land in a different house. Great when you WANT a fresh shuffle (a new run), catastrophic if someone does it mid-experiment.
40 shoppers, salt = "aK3x9"
💄 AmpliBeauty: Two weeks into the checkout test, a well-meaning engineer edits the salt "to clean things up." Instantly, thousands of shoppers silently swap variants — people mid-purchase see the other checkout, the data mixes both experiences, and the experiment is unreadable. The right use: when instrumentation was broken, start a new run — a deliberate fresh shuffle with previous data excluded.

How it works in Amplitude

  • Each flag gets a random salt at creation; it feeds the bucketing hash.
  • Changing it mid-flight causes users to switch variants — Amplitude gates salt changes behind approvals for a reason.
  • Legit uses: fresh randomization for a rerun, or de-correlating two experiments that share an audience.

When a reshuffle is RIGHT

  • A new experiment run after fixing broken instrumentation — fresh shuffle, previous data excluded.
  • De-correlating two experiments that share an audience (same salt = correlated assignments).

📌 Sticky Bucketing

"Keep the variant you first saw"Feature Experiment ✅ / Web Experiment ❌
🧒 ELI6: You keep the toy you started with. Even if the teacher changes the rules about who gets which toy, kids who already have one keep theirs. Only kids arriving after the rule change follow the new rules. No child bursts into tears because their toy got swapped mid-play.
💄 AmpliBeauty: They ramp a VIP-pricing test from 10% to 50% traffic. Without stickiness, early shoppers can get re-dealt — Priya saw the bundle at $39 on Monday and $45 on Thursday. Cue screenshots, support tickets, and a very awkward "price glitch" thread. Sticky bucketing locks Priya to her original price while new traffic fills the new split.

How it works in Amplitude

  • Checks whether the user already experienced a variant (stored as a user property); if yes, serves the last variant they saw.
  • Protects against variant jumping from targeting/allocation changes.
  • Trade-off: users no longer strictly follow current allocation — turning it on/off mid-test can distort exposure ratios (SRM risk), so decide before launch.

Fine print

  • Works with remote evaluation only — local evaluation doesn't support sticky bucketing (evaluation modes → Act 2 🚚).
  • Stores the last-seen variant as a user property.
  • Not available in Web Experiment; pick your stickiness strategy before launch, not during.

🚚 Act 2 — The variant reaches her

Delivery mechanics: which keys unlock which surfaces, who computes the decision, and where your code runs.

🚀 Deployments

Named keys that connect flags to app surfacesClient keys vs server keys
🧒 ELI6: Name badges for each door of your shop — the website door, the app door, and the kitchen door in the back. A flag is only handed out at doors you've badged. The kitchen badge (server key) is powerful and stays secret; front-door badges (client keys) are safe to show in public. Wrong badge at the wrong door? Nothing gets through.
One experiment, three surfaces, three keys
💄 AmpliBeauty: Three deployments: web-storefront (client), ios-app (client), pricing-service (server). The margin-sensitive dynamic-pricing experiment is attached only to pricing-service — so the web bundle can never leak tomorrow's price logic into view-source, and each surface's data stays cleanly attributed to its deployment key.

How it works in Amplitude

  • A deployment = a named API key belonging to a project; you attach flags/experiments to deployments to deliver them.
  • Client deployments ship in browsers/apps (visible, limited); server deployments stay in your backend (secret, required for local evaluation).
  • The key also stamps collected data, keeping results attribution clean.

Key hygiene

  • Never embed a server key in client code — it's readable by anyone with dev tools.
  • One deployment per surface/environment keeps the blast radius of a mistake small.
  • The key stamps collected data, so per-surface attribution stays clean.

🌍 Local vs Remote Evaluation — WHO decides the variant

Remote: Amplitude's servers decide, enrichedLocal: your SDK decides in-process, sub-millisecond
🧒 ELI6: Asking HQ vs owning the rulebook. Remote = phone HQ every time: "what should this shopper see?" HQ knows everything about them (their history, their groups) but the call takes time. Local = you keep a synced copy of the rulebook and answer instantly yourself — lightning fast, but YOU must bring the shopper's details, because nobody at HQ is looking them up for you.
💄 AmpliBeauty: The homepage hero test targets the "Lapsed VIPs" cohort → remote evaluation, because Amplitude's servers enrich the request with cohort membership, geolocation and historical user properties. Meanwhile the search-ranking experiment handles 30k requests/sec → local evaluation: flag configs sync to the SDK, each request evaluated in-process (Amplitude clocks JVM at 0.007ms). The trade they accept: locally there's no sticky bucketing, no enrichment, and cohorts match by User ID only.

Remote gives you

  • Amplitude ID resolution + user enrichment: IP geolocation, canonicalized platform/language, historical user properties, transformed properties.
  • Cohort targeting from Analytics (dynamic cohorts sync hourly — don't use for time-sensitive targeting).
  • Sticky bucketing — remote only.

Local gives you (and costs you)

  • Sub-millisecond, zero network per user — flag configs poll in the background; Node local eval can even run at the edge.
  • ❌ No ID resolution, no enrichment, no sticky bucketing; cohorts by User ID only (Node/JVM/Go/Python/Ruby, not PHP).
  • Server-side local tracks assignment events, not exposures — use assignment as the exposure event in analysis.

🧭 Client-side vs Server-side Experimentation — WHERE your code runs

Two independent questions → a 2×2 matrixAll 4 combos are real in Amplitude
🧒 ELI6: People mash two questions into one. Question 1: where does your code run? In the shopper's hands (client = browser, app) or in your kitchen (server = backend). Question 2: who decides the variant? HQ (remote) or your own rulebook copy (local). They mix freely — 4 combos, each with a superpower and a trap. Confusing them is like mixing up "where the chef stands" with "who wrote the recipe."
🧠 The 2-question chooser
Q1 — WHERE does the tested experience live? A shopper could screenshot the difference (banner, copy, layout) → CLIENT. Invisible logic (pricing, ranking, API behavior) → SERVER. This one is decided for you by what you're testing.
Q2 — Brains or speed? Need cohorts, geolocation, historical properties, sticky bucketing → REMOTE. Need sub-millisecond, high QPS, zero network → LOCAL. This one you choose.
Memory hook: WHAT you test picks client or server. Brains or speed picks remote or local.
💄 AmpliBeauty runs all four quadrants. Press play — each ~7-second story shows one quadrant in action, with the payoff and the gotcha.

How to choose

  • Need cohorts, geo, or historical user properties? → remote.
  • Need sub-ms at scale or edge workers? → server + local.
  • UI test with rich targeting and auto exposure tracking? → client + remote (the default for a reason).
  • Client + local is the niche pick: instant evaluation, no fetch — accept the visibility trade-off.

Common gotchas

  • ⚠️ Client + local exposes targeting data: target someone by email and every client can read that email in the flag config.
  • Server + local: no automatic exposure events — use the assignment event as your exposure event in analysis.
  • Remote dynamic cohorts sync hourly (up to 10M users) — pass user properties explicitly for time-sensitive targeting. Local cohort sync matches by User ID only (up to 50M).
  • Sticky bucketing works with remote only — plan ramps accordingly.

⚖️ Act 3 — AmpliBeauty reads the verdict

The stats layer: the significance verdict, the p-value machinery underneath it, and the confidence net around the lift.

⚖️ Statistical Significance

The verdict: "too weird to be luck"Amplitude badge appears at ≥95% (α = 0.05)
🧒 ELI6: A courtroom verdict. "Statistically significant" means the evidence is now strong enough that "it was just luck" stops being a reasonable defense. And evidence comes from exactly two places: how big the effect is and how many witnesses (users) you have. A whisper can be significant in a quiet room with many listeners; a shout can fail in a storm with three. Drag the sliders below and watch the verdict change.
Lift Users
💄 AmpliBeauty: The lipstick shade recommender shows +0.4pt conversion on 4,000 shoppers → 78% significance. "Almost 95!" is not a verdict — it's an unfinished trial; keep running. Three weeks later at 80,000 shoppers the same lift reads 98% ✓. Meanwhile the gift-wrap upsell jumps +12% and hits significance in 3 days on just 6,000 shoppers. Same bar, two roads: big effects convict fast, small effects need a bigger jury.

Reading it in Amplitude

  • Significance = 1 − p-value; the "Statistically significant" badge appears when it clears 95%.
  • Under the default sequential engine it's always-valid — the day it's cleared, you may call it.
  • Cross-check: the confidence interval clearing zero and the badge tell the same story.

The classic traps

  • Statistically significant ≠ business significant: with enough users a +0.05pp lift gets the badge and still isn't worth shipping — read the CI edges.
  • Not significant ≠ no effect — often just not enough evidence yet (underpowered).
  • 94.9% is not "basically 95" — the bar exists precisely so hope can't move it.

🎲 P-Value (what p = 0.05 actually means)

Amplitude shows significance = 1 − pConclusive when p < 0.05
🧒 ELI6: "How surprised should I be?" Pretend your change did absolutely nothing, and imagine running the experiment 100 times anyway — pure luck still produces small "wins" and "losses" every time. The p-value asks: out of those 100 do-nothing experiments, how many would look as impressive as mine by luck alone? p = 0.05 means only ~5 would. Your result is too weird to be luck — so we believe it's real.
💄 AmpliBeauty: The Shade Finder quiz shows +0.5pt conversion with p = 0.008 → if the quiz truly did nothing, luck would beat this result in fewer than 1 in 100 tries. Ship it. The same test's "wishlist adds" metric shows +2% with p = 0.34 → luck produces that all the time. Shrug, don't tweet it.

Reading it in Amplitude

  • Analysis view shows significance = 1 − p-value (e.g. p = 0.03 → 97% significance).
  • Default sequential testing means p-values are always-valid — safe to peek daily.
  • With multiple variants/metrics, Bonferroni raises the bar per test.

The classic trap

  • p = 0.05 does NOT mean "95% chance the variant is better" — that sentence belongs to Bayesian's P(beat control).
  • It means: if there were no real effect, a result this extreme happens 5% of the time.
  • Not significant ≠ "no effect" — it can mean "not enough data yet."

📏 Confidence Interval

The range of plausible liftsExcludes 0 → statistically significant
🧒 ELI6: Not a single number — a net. "The true lift is somewhere inside this net, and nets built this way catch the truth about 95 times out of 100." Early on the net is huge (could be anything); more data weaves it tighter. If the entire net sits above zero, you can celebrate — even the worst edge of it is still a win.
💄 AmpliBeauty: Free-sampler test, revenue lift CI = [+0.8%, +4.2%]. Whole net above zero → significant. Better: even the pessimistic edge (+0.8%) covers the sampler program's cost, so finance signs off on the worst case. Compare the loyalty-badge test: CI = [−1.1%, +4.9%] — the net straddles zero, could genuinely be a loss. Keep running or accept uncertainty; don't declare victory.

Reading it in Amplitude

  • Shown per metric per variant around the lift; under default sequential testing the CI starts wide and tightens (the "peeking tax").
  • Decision rule: CI fully above 0 → ship signal; fully below 0 → regression; straddles 0 → not conclusive yet.

The classic trap

  • "95% CI" doesn't mean 95% of users improved — it's about how often such nets capture the true value across repeated experiments.
  • A significant-but-tiny lift can still be commercially meaningless: read the interval's edges, not just the star.

🔮 Epilogue — Don't start trials you can't finish

The Duration Estimator inverts Act 3: given the verdict you want, how much evidence do you need — and do you even have the traffic to collect it?

📐 The Duration Estimator

Pre-launch viability check, inside experiment setupAuto-pulls ~30 days of real traffic + baseline from Analytics
🧒 ELI6: A fuel check before a road trip. How far is the destination? (MDE — small effects live far away.) How fast is your car? (traffic per day.) How sure do you want to be of arriving? (confidence.) The clever part: instead of asking you to guess your speed, it reads your actual fuel history — the last ~30 days of real traffic and conversion from Analytics. Some destinations turn out to be 632 days away. Those trips you simply don't take.
Traffic/day Baseline conv.
💄 AmpliBeauty triages the backlog: Three ideas, one Duration Scenarios table. Hero banner redesign (expect ~8% lift) → about 7 weeks at 90% confidence: run it. CTA copy tweak (~5%) → about 4 months: viable only at 85% confidence for a directional read. Footer link color (~2%) → 632 days: politely deleted from the backlog, and the PM who suggested it buys coffee. That's the estimator's real job — not predicting, prioritizing.

What makes it powerful

  • Grounded in your own data: pulls ~29–30 days of traffic and the live baseline conversion for your chosen metric — no spreadsheet guessing.
  • The Duration Scenarios table (confidence × MDE) turns it into a backlog prioritizer: kill unviable tests before engineering builds them.
  • Levers when a test runs too long: bigger MDE, lower confidence (when stakes allow), higher-traffic page, fuller rollout, fewer variants.

The fine print

  • T-tests only — sequential, Bayesian and bandit methods aren't part of this pre-launch workflow.
  • It's a different tool from the live in-flight duration estimate on a running experiment (which uses sequential testing + observed data) — the two numbers won't match, and that's expected.
  • An estimate, not a promise: accuracy depends on the proxy event you pick and on recent traffic staying representative.
