⚡ At a glance: who has what

Same statistical toolbox, different defaults. The biggest philosophical difference: Amplitude runs sequential testing by default (peek anytime), while Statsig defaults to fixed-horizon frequentist and offers sequential, SPRT and Bayesian as opt-in modes.

📐 T-Test (and Z-Test)

Statsig: default engine (z-test, Welch's t for small n)Amplitude: Welch's T-test (fixed-horizon option)
🧒 ELI6: You and your friend each have a bag of cookies, and yours look bigger. But are they really bigger — or did you just happen to grab a big one? The T-test is the referee who checks ALL the cookies in both bags and rules: "yes, truly bigger" or "nah, same-ish, you got lucky." The bigger the difference and the more alike the cookies in each bag, the easier the call.
💄 AmpliBeauty: They test a new "Shade Finder" quiz on the foundation category page. Control converts at 4.1%, quiz variant at 4.6%. The T-test asks: given ~50k shoppers per arm and daily noise from payday spikes and K-beauty drops, is that +0.5pt lift signal or luck? p = 0.008 → signal. Ship the quiz.

Statsig

  • Default frequentist engine: two-sample z-test on metric deltas.
  • Switches to Welch's t-test for small samples.
  • Fixed-horizon by design — decide sample size up front, read at the end.

Amplitude

  • Welch's T-test available as the fixed-horizon method.
  • Doesn't assume equal variance between arms (Welch's).
  • Default experience is sequential instead — see below.

🎧 CUPED (Variance Reduction)

Statsig: CUPED + CURE (Warehouse Native)Amplitude: CUPED (advanced settings)
🧒 ELI6: Kids run a race to test new shoes. Problem: some kids are just naturally fast, so it's hard to tell if the shoes did anything. CUPED's trick: check how fast each kid ran last week, then ask one simple question — did they beat their own usual speed? Once everyone is compared to themselves, the shoes' real effect pops out much faster.
Raw data — wide confidence intervals
💄 AmpliBeauty: Revenue-per-user is dominated by VIP "beauty haulers" who spend $300/month regardless of what you test. In a free-sampler experiment, CUPED uses each shopper's last-30-day spend as a covariate: the haulers' predictable spending is netted out, confidence intervals shrink ~40%, and a test that needed 4 weeks reads out in ~2.5.

Statsig

  • Enabled per metric; configurable pre-exposure lookback window.
  • CURE on Warehouse Native extends CUPED with regression on multiple covariates.
  • Also used to detect/absorb pre-experiment bias.

Amplitude

  • Toggle in the experiment's advanced statistical settings.
  • Explicitly framed as the fix for pre-exposure bias from unbalanced bucketing.
  • Applied to supported metric types in analysis.

✂️ Winsorization

Statsig: percentile capping per metric (+ exact caps on WHN)Amplitude: percentile capping + log transform
🧒 ELI6: One kid brings 500 marbles to school, and suddenly "the average kid has 40 marbles" — which is nonsense. Winsorization says: for the math, pretend that kid brought 20 (about the most a normal kid brings). Nobody takes their marbles away — we just don't let one kid single-handedly wreck the average.
Raw: 2 whales inflate the mean
💄 AmpliBeauty: During a bundle-pricing test, a professional makeup artist buys $8,400 of stock in one order — landing in the treatment arm. Without winsorization, treatment "wins" on revenue purely because of her. Cap at P99 (~$450/order) and the reseller's order counts as $450: now the test measures what typical shoppers actually do.

Statsig

  • Percentile computed on non-zero, non-null values; everything above Pₓ set to Pₓ.
  • Warehouse Native: configurable per metric, plus exact value capping (e.g. cap daily purchases at 100 to exclude resellers).

Amplitude

  • Configurable winsorization percentile per experiment metric.
  • Optional log transform for heavy-tailed metrics — winsorization applies first, then the log.
  • Pairs with bot-blocking / internal-user filtering.

🎯 Bonferroni Correction

Statsig: optional (also offers Benjamini–Hochberg)Amplitude: applied for multi-variant/metric; off in Bayesian
🧒 ELI6: Make one wish and it comes true — wow, magic! Make 12 wishes and one comes true — that's not magic, that's just odds. Checking 12 metrics is making 12 wishes: one will "win" by pure luck. Bonferroni's rule: the more wishes you make, the stronger the proof each one needs before anyone believes it.
💄 AmpliBeauty: The growth PM launches one homepage test but attaches 12 metrics: revenue, AOV, quiz starts, wishlist adds, reviews read... At α = 0.05, there's a ~46% chance at least one metric "wins" by pure chance. Bonferroni sets each metric's bar to 0.05/12 ≈ 0.004 — the fake "wishlist adds +9%!" win disappears, and only real effects survive.

Statsig

  • Optional; adjusted α shown on hover over confidence intervals.
  • Can correct across variants and/or metrics; also applies in power analysis.
  • Offers Benjamini–Hochberg as a less-strict FDR alternative — Amplitude has no direct equivalent.

Amplitude

  • Treats each variant/metric as its own hypothesis; correction guards the family-wise error rate.
  • Automatically disabled under Bayesian analysis (Bayesian controls false positives differently).

👀 Sequential Testing

Statsig: opt-in toggle (regression detection)Amplitude: the DEFAULT stats engine
🧒 ELI6: Baking a cake the old way: open the oven early and you ruin it. Sequential testing is an oven with a glass door — peek as often as you like, nothing gets ruined. The catch: early on the glass is foggy, so you can only call it if the result is REALLY obvious. The longer you wait, the clearer the glass gets.
💄 AmpliBeauty: They ship a new one-page checkout the week before a mega sale. With Amplitude's default sequential engine, the team peeks daily with a clear conscience. Day 6: adjusted CI on checkout conversion excludes zero → call it early and lock in the win before the sale. And if it had tanked conversion, the same math would have flagged the regression by day 2–3 instead of week 4.

Statsig

  • Opt-in overlay on frequentist results: adjusted CIs shown alongside unadjusted.
  • Positioned mainly for early regression detection, not early shipping.
  • If the adjusted CI still overlaps zero — keep running as planned.

Amplitude

  • Default methodology: always-valid p-values, flexible sample size.
  • Peek any time without inflating false positive rates.
  • Trade-off: with small lifts, significance can take longer than a fixed-horizon test.

🏁 SPRT (Sequential Probability Ratio Test)

Statsig: dedicated analytics typeAmplitude: mSPRT powers its sequential engine
🧒 ELI6: A tug-of-war between "it works" and "it doesn't." Every new shopper's data pulls the rope a little one way or the other. The instant the rope crosses a line — game over, we have our answer. A strong team wins in seconds; two even teams keep pulling. That's why obvious winners finish fast and unclear tests keep going.
💄 AmpliBeauty: They A/B a "Only 3 left!" urgency badge on product pages. The effect is big and immediate — add-to-bag jumps. Under SPRT the evidence ratio smashes through the decision boundary in 5 days on a test scoped for 3 weeks. That's 16 days of extra conversion banked, and the test slot freed for the next idea.

Statsig

  • Explicit, selectable analytics type in experiment setup.
  • Meaningfully shortens tests when evidence is strong in either direction.
  • Traditional methods may still be preferable for some experiment types.

Amplitude

  • Not a separate mode — its default sequential testing is mSPRT (mixture SPRT), the same family.
  • So "does Amplitude have SPRT?" → yes, it's the engine under the hood.

🔮 Bayesian Statistics

Statsig: Bayesian analytics type + priorsAmplitude: Bayesian option — P(beat control)
🧒 ELI6: Guess how many candies are in the jar. Someone shows you a handful — you fix your guess. Another handful — you fix it again. Start unsure, end confident. That's Bayesian: every bit of data updates your best guess. And instead of cryptic stats-speak, it answers like a weather forecast: "94% chance the new version is better."
Prior: vague belief around zero lift
💄 AmpliBeauty: The CMO won't read p-values. For a loyalty-tier renaming test, the team switches to Bayesian and reports: "92% probability the new names lift repeat purchase; expected downside if we're wrong is 0.1%." Decision made in one meeting. Bonus: they encode a prior from last year's similar rebrand test, so the experiment starts smart instead of from scratch.

Statsig

  • Selectable analytics type; supports informative priors (posterior precision-weights prior + observed data).
  • Good for encoding institutional knowledge from past tests.

Amplitude

  • Bayesian option reports probability-to-beat-control and expected loss.
  • Controls false positives its own way — Bonferroni is auto-disabled in this mode.

🚨 SRM (Sample Ratio Mismatch)

Statsig: auto chi-squared health checkAmplitude: auto chi-squared diagnostic
🧒 ELI6: You split candies 50/50 between two friends — but one somehow ends up with way more. With 10 candies, that can happen by luck. With a million candies, it can't — someone is dropping candies on the way. SRM is the alarm that catches the leak. And rule one: when candies are leaking, don't judge who's happier — fix the leak first.
💄 AmpliBeauty: A landing-page redirect test shows treatment winning big — but SRM fires: 55/45 split. Cause: the redirect took 1.2s and impatient mobile users on slow SEA networks bounced before the treatment exposure logged. The "win" was survivorship bias — only patient users stayed in treatment. Without the SRM check, they'd have shipped a page that actually loses mobile revenue.

Statsig

  • Automatic chi-squared test of observed vs expected allocation.
  • Tiered alerts by p-value severity; also checks deduplication bias across ID types on WHN.

Amplitude

  • Automatic SRM diagnostic with a dedicated troubleshooting guide.
  • Docs call out common causes: mid-flight allocation changes, redirect timing, targeting changes.
