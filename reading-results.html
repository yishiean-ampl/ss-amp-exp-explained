🧒 Explain like I'm 6

The scorecard is your report card 📋. Lift = how much better the new thing did. CI = the teacher saying "your real grade is somewhere between B− and A+". p-value = how likely you'd see this grade by pure luck if you learned nothing. Small p = probably not luck.

Statistical vs practical significance

Statistically significant (CI excludes zero) = the effect is real. Practically significant = the effect is big enough to matter. A real-but-tiny +0.1% lift may not be worth the engineering cost. You need both to ship happily. ✌️

⚠️ Trap

"p = 0.10, so the feature had no effect. Kill it."

tap 👆
✅ Reality

p > 0.05 means "not enough data to confirm" — not "the effect is zero." A +12% lift with CI [−3%, +27%] is an underpowered result, not a null one. It needs more time or traffic.

🕺 Unit 2 · Participation Logic (the #1 gotcha)

The single most misunderstood concept in Statsig — and the #1 source of support tickets. Watch it happen:

💌 20 users get assigned (exposed) … how many participate?
ASSIGNED (Diagnostics "exposures")
0
PARTICIPATED (fired the metric event AFTER exposure)
0
🧒 Explain like I'm 6

You invited 20 kids to your party 💌 (assigned). Only 3 actually came and danced 🕺 (participated). The party isn't broken — most invited kids just didn't come. If your metric is "checkout completed", most users simply never check out. Participation = assignments × how often the event fires.

🧮 Real numbers from the course: 140,000 exposures, 2,100 purchase participants = 1.5% purchase rate. Totally normal for e-commerce. Only participants count toward the metric math.

⏳ Bake windows — yesterday's data is still cooking

The last 24–48 hours of any metric chart look artificially flat because events are still being processed. "The metric dropped to zero this morning!" 😱 → 🎂 it's still baking. Report only on baked data.

🅰️ Amplitude bridge

Statsig Diagnostics "exposures" ≈ Amplitude Experiment's "assignment". Statsig participation has no exact Amplitude twin — closest idea is users with the metric event post-exposure ("metric exposure" in some contexts). The bake window concept maps 1:1. 🟢

🧒 Explain like I'm 6

You sold lemonade on one street corner 🍋 and made $6. The Impact tab says "if you set up on every corner in town, you'd make $120!" Maybe — but you haven't earned $120. It's a forecast, not a receipt. 🧾

🗣️ Safe sentence: "The Impact tab projects 50K subscriptions if we ship to 100%." Unsafe sentence: "Our experiment proved we can add 50K." Extrapolation ≠ proof.
⚠️ Trap

Pulse shows "infinite relative delta" on a ratio metric. Panic? ♾️

tap 👆
✅ Reality

The ratio's denominator CI crosses zero, so the ratio's CI is mathematically infinite. Display edge case, not a data error. Read the absolute per-user metric instead.

⚠️ Trap

"The Pulse UI count doesn't match my warehouse query — data loss?!"

✅ Reality

The UI uses HLL++ sketches — a fast approximation (~0.2–0.3% error) for count-distinct. Warehouse queries are exact. A small gap is expected; big gaps usually come from timing windows, not the approximation.

Not every anomaly is a bad result — some are bugs. Diagnostics first, scorecard second.

🚨 SRM DETECTED — allocation compromised

A 46/54 split "close enough"? No — if the chi-squared test says this imbalance has <0.01% chance of being random, something systematic is wrong: crash-before-exposure bugs, bot traffic, conditional logging, truncated logs. More data makes it worse, not better — you're adding more biased data. Diagnostic: check the SRM p-value time series (noisy = maybe false alarm; trending to 0 = real bug), use the SRM Debugger, fix, restart.

🐋 Whales & wide CIs — why your CI won't shrink

Most users spend a little… one whale spends a LOT. Watch the average jump.
avg 💰

Heavy-tailed metrics (marketplaces, freemium revenue) have huge variance → wide CIs even with a perfect split and no SRM. It looks like the platform is broken; it's just statistics. Fixes: check whether CUPED applied shows on the metric row (variance already cut ~30–50%), run longer, get more traffic, or switch to a higher-frequency proxy metric.

⏱️ Watch a CI narrow with more data

Same true effect (+2.5%). More days → tighter interval → readable direction.
Day 2 — CI [−6.4%, +11.4%] · crosses zero → can't call it 🤷
🧒 Explain like I'm 6

Guessing how many candies are in a jar 🍬: after 2 peeks your guess is "somewhere between 50 and 500" (useless). After 100 peeks it's "between 180 and 220" (useful!). p > 0.05 doesn't mean "no candy" — it means you haven't looked enough yet.

⚖️ Unit 5 · Making the Call

Scorecard clean, no SRM, participation sane. Now: ship, keep running, or kill? Practice on 3 scenarios — no stakes.

🧭 The synthesis routine: start with the primary → check secondaries are consistent → walk every guardrail. Clear primary win + all guardrails within tolerance = ship. Statistically real guardrail regression = investigate first. Primary CI crossing zero + time left = keep running.
📋 Scenario 1: Primary +3.0% CI [+0.5%, +5.5%] ✅ · Error-rate guardrail +2.0% CI [+0.1%, +3.9%] ⚠️ · Both p < 0.05. PM says "primary is clearly positive, ship it."
🎯 Investigate first. Both CIs exclude zero — the guardrail regression is as statistically real as the win. Three defensible paths: ship-and-fix (cause known & benign), ship-and-instrument, or hold. "Ship because primary is stronger" is not one of them.
📋 Scenario 2: Primary +12% CI [−3%, +27%], p=0.10. Ran 6 of 14 planned days. Manager: "not significant — kill it."
🎯 Keep running. p > 0.05 means "not enough data to confirm", not "effect is zero." The point estimate is large and the planned duration isn't done. Let it mature to the pre-committed horizon, then call it.
📋 Scenario 3: Primary +2.1% CI [+0.8%, +3.4%] ✅ · all guardrails flat, CIs hugging zero · ran full planned duration · secondaries consistent.
🎯 Ship. This is the clean case: real primary win, consistent secondaries, guardrails quiet, full duration honored. Endless re-running has a cost too (opportunity cost + multiple-testing risk). Make the call. 🎉
