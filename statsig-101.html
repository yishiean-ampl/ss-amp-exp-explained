🧒 Explain like I'm 6

You want to know if your new cookie recipe 🍪 is better. If you only give it to your best friends (targeting rule), of course they say it's great — they were already your fans! Only a coin flip decides fairly, because it makes both groups the same before the cookie. That's why: gate metrics = hints, experiment = proof.

💙 Gates: boolean on/off, Pass % ramping (any split, never reshuffles assigned users), kill switch. Experiments: random assignment, JSON variant configs, Allocation % with splits capped 50/50, Pulse scorecard. Sequential tools in one loop — never interchangeable.

🅰️ Fine print from the docs: Statsig does balance the gate's fail group ("Fail – Not in Analysis" bucket) for gate metrics, which helps — but for a ship decision on measured impact, run an experiment. Statsig↔Amplitude term mapping lives in your Concept Map artifact. 🪨

🧒 Explain like I'm 6

Every kid gets a permanent raffle number written on their hand 🎟️. The gate says "numbers under 30 win." Same kid, same number, same result — forever (that's deterministic hashing). But you don't get to CHOOSE the numbers — so your test account might just have ticket #87. Want it in anyway? Use an override — the VIP list that skips the raffle. 📝

🪪 Name tag vs wristband

User ID 🏷️ = logged-in users. Stable ID 🎗️ = anonymous users — SDK-generated, stored on the device (local storage), survives without a login. The trap: using User ID on a pre-login surface makes everyone the same "null" user and the gate silently misbehaves. Pre-auth = Stable ID, always.

🎲 Lab 1 · What a p-value ACTUALLY is

Your experiment showed treatment winning by +4%. Question: could pure luck do that? Let's run 100 fake experiments where NOTHING changed (both arms identical) and see how often luck alone produces a gap ≥ 4%:

Each square = one "nothing changed" experiment. 🟨 = luck alone produced ≥ +4% anyway.
—
Press play to run 100 null experiments.
🧒 Explain like I'm 6

Your friend flips a coin 10 times and gets 9 heads, then says "I have magic powers!" 🪄 You check: how often does a NORMAL coin give 9+ heads? About 1% of the time. That 1% is the p-value — the chance that boring old luck explains the "magic." Small p = probably not luck. p = 0.05 means luck would only pull this off 5 times in 100.

📏 Lab 2 · What "95% confident" really means

A 95% CI is NOT "95% chance the truth is in this bar." It's about the recipe: if you re-ran the experiment many times, the CI recipe would capture the true effect ~19 times out of 20. Watch 20 re-runs:

Green line = the TRUE effect (+2.5%, normally invisible to you!). Each bar = one experiment's 95% CI.
~19 of 20 bars will catch the green line. The unlucky red one? That's the 5% — and you never know which one you got. Humility, statistically enforced. 🙏
🧒 Explain like I'm 6

You throw 20 hula hoops 🪀 at a hidden flag pole. Your throwing technique is good enough that ~19 hoops land around the pole. Any ONE hoop probably rings it — but one of them missed, and you can't tell which. The CI is the hoop, not the pole.

👀 Lab 3 · Why peeking burns you

This experiment has NO real effect (p should stay high). Watch what its p-value does day by day anyway — pure noise wiggle:

The p-value of a NULL experiment over 14 days. Red zone = "significant" (p < 0.05).

Days 6–7 the noise dipped into the red zone. Peek daily and stop at the first dip → you ship a feature that did nothing.

🏀 Lab 3b · Peeker vs Patient — same experiments, different rule

🧒 Explain like I'm 6

Two equally good basketball teams play. At SOME point in almost every game, one team is briefly ahead — that's just how games flow. Now imagine you're allowed to end the game the moment your team is ahead 🏀⏱️. You'd "win" most games… even though the teams are equal. That's peeking. The final buzzer (fixed horizon) is what makes the score mean something.

🧯 Same data, same experiments — the only difference was the stopping rule. Fixes: pre-commit to a duration (sample needed per arm ÷ daily traffic = days, via the power analysis in setup) and only judge at the buzzer; or enable Sequential Testing (Setup → Frequentist → Analysis Settings, toggleable mid-run) — it widens early CIs ("wings") so checking early stays honest, at the cost of needing stronger evidence to call it early.

📊 Unit 4 · Learn: Reading Pulse

Three concepts trip up every new reader: participation, the CI-zero rule, bake windows.

💌 Participation ≠ assignment

Assigned (Diagnostics "exposures") = invited to the party. Participated = showed up AND danced 🕺 (fired the qualifying metric event after exposure). Only participants count in the metric math. 28,000 exposures with 3,200 participants isn't broken — it's a normal event-fire rate. (Want the animated dots version? It's in your Reading Results artifact, Unit 2.)

⏳ Bake windows — watch yesterday's data finish cooking

A daily metric chart. The newest days LOOK collapsed… until the data bakes.
The last 24–48h of any metric chart is still processing. "It dropped to zero this morning!" 😱 → 🎂 no, it's just raw. Never report on unbaked data.

📏 The CI-zero reading rule

Pulse showsMeaningCall
CI [+1.1%, +4.6%]Whole range positive — direction is trustworthy✅ Real win
CI [−1.2%, +4.8%]Could be up, flat, or down — Lab 2's hoop still includes zero🤷 Keep running
CI [−4.9%, −0.8%]Whole range negative❌ Real loss
📐 The rule: if the CI crosses zero, don't trust the direction. A positive point estimate inside a zero-crossing CI is a forecast of "−1°C to +5°C" — you still don't know if you need a jacket. 🌦️
